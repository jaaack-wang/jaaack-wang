I am a recent MA graduate in Applied Linguistics from the University of Saskatchewan. My main research interests are in natural language processing, computational social science, and language technology. I employ both computational and linguistic approches to studying the properties of languages (i.e., English and Mandarin) and their linguistic and social meanings inferred from actual language use in large-scale linguistic corpora (already built or compiled through web scraping).

Inspired by Andrew Ng, I believe in the prospect of Data-centric AI (versus Model-centric AI). This is because with the maturing of deep learning frameworks and cloud computing, trying out different machine/deep learning models becomes generally much cheaper than having a well-designed dataset, and the qualifty of the dataset becomes a main reason that causes the performances of SOTA models plateau out. That implies, domain knowledge (plus some data science knowledge) in data construction and annotation will play an increasingly impotant role in real-world deep learning application. Natural language processing or computational linguistics is certainly not an exception.

Bio

I was born and raised in Fuqing, a county-level city in Fujian province, the southeastern coast of China. From 2015 to 2019, I studied at Hunan University for my Bachelor's degree. Majoring in Chinese Language and Literature, I found my passion for linguistics in the second semester of my first year in college and managed to undertake a two-year project on the construction of Chinese grammar, funded by the national Student Innovation Program. I was also fortunate to be selected for a summer internship at the University of Alberta for an experimental psycholinguistic research project.

Besides literature- and linguistics- related essays/papers, I spent considerable time writing essays in philosophy, history, and law during my bachelor's years. Prior to coming to Hunan University, I devoted most weekends of my last year in high school working on a 40-page long essay addressing Chinese notions of sex over time.

From 2019 to 2021, I studied at the University of Saskatchewan majoring in Applied Linguistics. I taught myself programming and started building computational tools, relying on linguistic knowledge (rule-based) and statistical learning algorithms, to carry out my research, which was about analysis of language use based in large-scale English corpora.

With more experiences in programming and mathematics, the recent months have seen me learning and participating in extensive machine/deep learning courses/training. I have shared many of my learning experiences as well as tools I built on my GitHub. You can also check out the Research and Resources sections to find out more.

Research

  • A macroscopic re-examination of language and gender: a corpus-based case study in the university classroom setting
    MA thesis, University of Saskatchewan, 2021
    About: The thesis compared the use of 98 lexico-grammatical linguistic features by university male and female instructors across 4 academic disciplines. The extraction of these features is automated by an open-source corpus-linguistic tool created in this study, called Linguistic Feature Extractor .

  • A proposal for unfilled pause reclassification in spontaneous English speech
    Zhengxiang Wang, Zhi Li, Ziwei Zhou (Manuscript in preparation), 2021
    About: Based in the British Academic Spoken English corpus , we proposed a new classification scheme for unfilled pauses. Unfilled Pause Classifier , a rule-based syntactic parser, was built for that purpose. The classifier achieved 86.7% agreement rates among the three researchers, and 80~86% test set accuracy and about 90% train set accuracy using Naive Bayes algorithms.

  • Grammar as science: Rethinking the construction of Modern Chinese Grammar (in Chinese)
    A funded research project, Hunan University, 2017-2019
    About: 11 essays/papers with one published, containing both synchronic and diachronic studies of several Chinese grammatical phenomena, such as part of speech classification, lexical reduplication, grammaticalization, negators, and discourse particles etc. The published paper , is a discussion on the sentence-based theory postulated by a pioneering Mondern Chinese grammar book written in 1924.

  • How does Chinese learner of English perceive and comprehend phonetic reduction
    A funded internship project, University of Alberta, 2018
    About: This project investigated the effects of phonetic reduction in casual spoken English on Chinese learners of English by a psycholinguistic experiment run on E-prime 2. The stimuli were manually extracted from a corpus of spontaneous English using Praat.
