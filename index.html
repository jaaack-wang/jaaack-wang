I am a recent MA graduate in Applied Linguistics from the University of Saskatchewan. My main research interests are in natural language processing, corpus linguistics, and computational social science. I employ both computational and linguistic approches to studying the properties of languages (i.e., English and Mandarin) and their linguistic and social meanings inferred from actual language use in large-scale linguistic corpora.

Inspired by Andrew Ng, I believe in the prospect of data-centric AI (versus model-centric AI). This is because with the maturing of deep learning frameworks and cloud computing, trying out different machine/deep learning models becomes generally much cheaper than having a well-designed dataset, and the qualifty of the dataset becomes a main reason that causes the performances of SOTA models to plateau out. That implies, domain knowledge (plus some data science knowledge) in data construction, annotation and augmentation will play an increasingly impotant role in real-world deep learning applications. Natural language processing or computational linguistics is certainly not an exception.

Bio

I was born and raised in Fuqing, a county-level city in Fujian province, the southeastern coast of China. From 2015 to 2019, I studied at Hunan University for my Bachelor's degree. Majoring in Chinese Language and Literature, I found my passion for linguistics in the first year of university and managed to undertake a two-year funded project on the construction of Chinese grammar. I was also fortunate to be selected for a summer internship at the University of Alberta for an experimental psycholinguistic research project.

Besides literature- and linguistics- related essays/papers, I spent considerable time doing research in law, history, social science, and a bit of western philosophy during my bachelor's years. Prior to coming to Hunan University, I devoted most weekends of my last year in high school working on a 40-page long essay addressing diachronic notions of sex of Chinese, which I see as the very beginning of my academic career. Some of my writings are shared on Misc.

From 2019 to 2021, I studied at the University of Saskatchewan majoring in Applied Linguistics. I taught myself programming and computational linguistics from scratch during this period of time when I focused on quantitative and data-driven analysis of language use in transcribed linguistic corpora. I have become an active user of GitHub since last year and so far I have open-sourced several text processing and annotating tools, a variety of linguistic datasets, as well as extensive notes and tutorials on deep learning in connection with a variety of NLP tasks. You are more than welcome to visit my GitHub or check out Resources to find out more.

Research

  • Linguistic Knowledge in Data Augmentation for Natural Language Processing: An Example on Chinese Question Matching
    Zhengxiang Wang, ArXiv:2111.14709v1 [cs.CL], 2021
    About: As an effort to data-centric NLP, I explored the role of linguistic knowledge in data augmentation for a binary Chinese question matching classification task. You can check out the source code, data, and experimental results on this GitHub repository .

  • A macroscopic re-examination of language and gender: a corpus-based case study in the university classroom setting
    MA thesis, University of Saskatchewan, 2021
    About: The thesis compared the use of 98 lexico-grammatical linguistic features by university male and female instructors across 4 academic disciplines. The extraction of these features is automated by an open-source corpus-linguistic tool created in this study, called Linguistic Feature Extractor .

  • A proposal for unfilled pause reclassification in spontaneous English speech
    Zhengxiang Wang, Zhi Li, Ziwei Zhou (Manuscript in preparation), 2021
    About: Based in the British Academic Spoken English corpus , we proposed a new classification scheme for unfilled pauses. Unfilled Pause Classifier , a rule-based syntactic parser, was built for that purpose. The classifier achieved 86.7% agreement rates among the three researchers, and 80~86% test set accuracy and about 90% train set accuracy when fitted into a Naive Bayes model.

  • Grammar as science: Rethinking the construction of Modern Chinese Grammar
    A funded research project, Hunan University, 2017-2019
    About: 11 essays/papers (in Chinese, 99 pages) with one published, containing both synchronic and diachronic studies of several Chinese grammatical phenomena, such as part of speech classification, lexical reduplication, grammaticalization, negators, and discourse particles etc. The published paper , is a discussion on the sentence-based theory postulated by a pioneering Mondern Chinese grammar book written in 1924.

  • How does Chinese learner of English perceive and comprehend phonetic reduction
    A funded internship project, University of Alberta, 2018
    About: This project investigated the effects of phonetic reduction in casual spoken English on Chinese learners of English by a psycholinguistic experiment run on E-prime 2. The stimuli were manually extracted from a corpus of spontaneous English using Praat.
